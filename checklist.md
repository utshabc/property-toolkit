# Building & Pest Inspection Report — Review Checklist

**Standards basis:** AS 4349.1 (Inspection of buildings — Pre-purchase inspections, residential buildings) and AS 4349.3 (timber pest inspections). Not legislated, but the industry-recognised standard most Victorian inspectors reference.

**Scope note for the tool:** Checks whether the report contains the structural elements a compliant AS 4349.1/4349.3 report should have, and flags severity language. Does not assess whether a defect is serious enough to walk away, or estimate repair costs.

| # | Checklist item | What it should contain | Why it matters to the buyer | Red flag / gap indicator |
|---|----------------|------------------------|------------------------------|---------------------------|
| 1 | Inspector independence | Report commissioned directly by (or for) the buyer, not supplied by the selling agent or vendor | Vendor-supplied reports carry a conflict-of-interest risk and may soften findings | Report was provided by the agent/vendor and buyer did not commission their own |
| 2 | Executive summary | Brief overview highlighting major defects and safety hazards found | Gives a fast, high-level read of the property's condition | No executive summary, or summary omits defects detailed later in the report |
| 3 | Scope & limitations | Areas inspected and NOT inspected (roof void, subfloor, obstructed areas, adjoining strata common property for units), and reasons for exclusion | Buyers often assume "no issues found" covers the whole property when large areas were inaccessible | No stated scope/limitations section, or scope excludes major areas without explanation |
| 4 | Standard compliance statement | Explicit statement that the inspection was conducted in accordance with AS 4349.1 (and AS 4349.3 for pest) | Confirms a recognised, consistent inspection methodology was used | No reference to AS 4349.1/4349.3 anywhere in the report |
| 5 | Major defects | Each major defect individually identified with location, description, and the inspector's opinion on why it's classified major | Major defects affect structural integrity or safety and are the most costly to remediate | Report uses vague language ("some issues noted") instead of individually identifying major defects |
| 6 | Minor defects | Overall extent of minor/maintenance-level defects described | Minor defects are usually cosmetic but cumulative cost should be visible | No mention of minor defects at all (unusually clean reports warrant a second look) |
| 7 | Safety hazards | Specific callouts for hazards such as deteriorated handrails, exposed wiring, unsafe balconies/stairs | Safety hazards may need urgent, non-negotiable remediation regardless of price negotiation | Visible hazard-prone features (old deck, exposed wiring) with no corresponding comment in the report |
| 8 | Pest/timber pest findings | Evidence of current or past termite/pest activity, damage extent, and conducive conditions (moisture, timber-to-ground contact) | Termite damage can be structurally serious and expensive; conducive conditions predict future risk | No pest inspection component at all, or "no evidence of pests" with no discussion of conducive conditions |
| 9 | Moisture/water damage | Findings from moisture meter readings, damp areas, drainage issues | Water ingress is a leading cause of long-term structural deterioration | Visible water staining/damp smell on inspection day but no moisture readings recorded |
| 10 | Illegal/unpermitted works cross-check | Comment on suspected owner-builder work, additions, or coverings that may hide damage or lack permits | Unpermitted work becomes the buyer's problem after settlement and may affect insurance | Obvious renovation/extension with no comment on permit status, unresolved against the Section 32's building permit disclosure |
| 11 | Photographic evidence | Photographs supporting major/minor defect findings, room by room | Substantiates findings and helps buyer/tradespeople assess severity independently | Findings described with no supporting photos |
| 12 | Recommendations | Suggested next steps — e.g. referral to a structural engineer, electrician, plumber, or pest controller for further assessment | Tells the buyer where professional follow-up is warranted before proceeding | Major defect noted with no recommendation for further specialist assessment |
| 13 | Report currency | Inspection date reasonably close to contract signing / end of due diligence period | Conditions (especially pests, moisture) can change; a stale report may not reflect current condition | Report dated significantly earlier than the contract date or due diligence deadline |
| 14 | Landlord/rental compliance items | Smoke alarm compliance, balustrade heights, pool fencing, and other items covered by the relevant state's minimum rental standards | These carry statutory deadlines and fines if the property is (or becomes) a rental — a different category of urgency from ordinary repair cost | Item present (e.g. smoke alarms noted but not tested) with no mention that it may carry a compliance deadline separate from repair cost |

## Note on apartments/units
For strata properties, AS 4349.1 scope is narrower — the inspector typically cannot access common property, the roof, or adjoining lots. A building & pest report on a unit should be read alongside the Owners Corporation Certificate/strata report, not as a substitute for it.
